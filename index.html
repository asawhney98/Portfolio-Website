Product Philosophy

These are the principles I keep returning to — shaped by shipping AI products in regulated financial services environments, managing platforms with $200M+ stakes, and building side projects entirely alone.

01
Ship the feedback loop, not just the feature

The most valuable product primitive is a fast, honest signal. Before I prioritise any feature, I ask: will this teach us something? In AI products especially, shipping a rough eval framework matters more than a polished UI no one uses. The product isn't the output — it's the learning rate.

02
Platforms are bets on other people's creativity

When I led the MaaS platform at Amex, the real metric wasn't how many models we onboarded — it was how many teams stopped asking us for help. A good platform makes you unnecessary in the best possible way. Platform PMs should be optimising for adoption without handholding.

03
Agentic AI needs a different risk contract

Shipping an LLM feature and shipping an agentic workflow are fundamentally different bets. Agents fail in novel ways — tool misuse, cascading errors, hallucinated confidence. The PM's job is to define the failure modes before the first demo. In regulated environments (credit, fraud, EU AI Act scope), the auditability of decisions is non-negotiable, not an afterthought.

04
Metrics are a compass, not a destination

I've shipped features that hit OKRs and hurt the product. DAU is easy to inflate. Retention tells you the truth. I've learned to be suspicious of metrics that move in the same direction as incentives, and to always pair a leading indicator with a lagging one that can contradict it.

05
The best roadmap decision is often a deletion

Roadmaps accumulate. Every sprint, scope grows. The hardest — and most underrated — PM skill is removing things: killing features, cutting scope, saying no to a stakeholder's pet idea. At Amex, reducing the offer→redemption funnel by removing eligibility friction outperformed three new features we had been building in parallel.

06
Cross-functional isn't a soft skill — it's infrastructure

You can have the right strategy and still fail if alignment is an afterthought. I've coordinated across 50+ Director teams. The ones that ship fast aren't the ones with the most talented people — they're the ones with the clearest decision rights, the fewest approval loops, and a shared definition of done.

My Frameworks & Thinking
On Evaluating Agentic AI Products

Traditional product metrics break with agents. You can't measure success with DAU alone when the agent operates autonomously. I use a 3-layer eval: Task completion rate (did it do the thing?), Confidence calibration (did it know when not to try?), and Audit traceability (can compliance reconstruct the decision path?). The third is often missing and always the most expensive to retrofit.

The MaaS Platform Adoption Curve

When I built MaaS at Amex, adoption followed a pattern I now call "the 40% cliff" — teams use a platform until it can't serve 40% of their edge cases, then they fork or abandon it. The fix isn't adding features. It's designing escape hatches — extension points that let teams customise without breaking the core contract. Platforms die from rigidity, not lack of functionality.

EU AI Act Implications for Fraud Risk PMs

If you're shipping AI in credit, fraud, or underwriting in the EU — you're likely in the high-risk classification under Annex III. This means auditability, data lineage, human oversight mechanisms, and continuous monitoring aren't optional. As a PM, you need to bake these into the PRD, not the compliance review. August 2026 deadline is the enforcement trigger for most FinTech use cases.
